# Reading Nutrition Labels

## Overview

Nutrition labels provide standardized information about the nutrient content of packaged foods. Understanding how to read them accurately enables comparison between products, identification of hidden sources of sodium and sugar, and informed decisions about portion sizes.

## Serving Size

The serving size is the reference unit for all nutrient information on the label. If the label lists 200 calories per serving and the package contains 2.5 servings, consuming the entire package delivers 500 calories. Serving sizes were updated in 2016 to reflect amounts people actually eat rather than aspirational portions. Checking serving size before reading any other figure is essential.

## Macronutrients

Total fat includes saturated fat, trans fat, and unsaturated fats. Saturated fat intake is associated with elevated LDL cholesterol; current guidelines recommend limiting it to less than 10% of daily calories. Trans fat should be minimized — a label can claim 0g trans fat while containing up to 0.5g per serving if partially hydrogenated oils appear in the ingredient list.

Carbohydrates include total sugars and dietary fiber. Total sugars combines naturally occurring sugars with added sugars. Added sugars — sucrose, high-fructose corn syrup, dextrose, and dozens of other synonyms — are explicitly listed after the 2016 label updates. Dietary fiber reduces the glycemic impact of carbohydrates and has independent health benefits.

## Percent Daily Value

Percent Daily Value (%DV) indicates how much of the daily recommended amount of a nutrient one serving provides, based on a 2,000-calorie reference diet. 5% DV or less is considered low; 20% DV or more is considered high. These benchmarks apply per nutrient per serving, not per product.

## Ingredient List

The ingredient list is ordered by weight, with the predominant ingredient first. A product where sugar appears as the first or second ingredient is primarily sugar regardless of how health-oriented the marketing appears. Familiar, pronounceable ingredients are not categorically healthier than chemical names — ascorbic acid is simply vitamin C — but the list's length is a rough proxy for the degree of processing.
