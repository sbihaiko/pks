# Healthy Aging Principles

## Overview

Healthy aging is not the absence of age-related change but the maintenance of functional capacity and quality of life despite biological senescence. Geroscience—the study of how aging drives disease—has identified modifiable factors that meaningfully extend healthspan, the period of life spent in good health.

## Biological Dimensions of Aging

### Cellular Senescence

Senescent cells accumulate with age and secrete inflammatory mediators that damage surrounding tissues. This senescence-associated secretory phenotype contributes to chronic low-grade inflammation—sometimes called inflammaging—that underlies many age-related diseases including cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and neurodegeneration.

### Telomere Biology

Telomeres, the protective caps on chromosomes, shorten with each cell division. Accelerated telomere shortening is associated with chronic stress, poor diet, sedentary behavior, and smoking. Lifestyle interventions that reduce oxidative stress appear to slow telomere attrition.

## Functional Domains

Healthy aging requires maintaining function across physical, cognitive, and social domains simultaneously. Decline in one domain often accelerates decline in others through interconnected pathways—physical inactivity worsens mood, which reduces social engagement, which in turn reduces cognitive stimulation.

### Cognitive Reserve

Cognitively stimulating activities throughout life build reserve that delays the symptomatic onset of neurodegenerative disease. Learning new skills—particularly those that are genuinely challenging and require sustained attention—is more beneficial than repeating familiar activities.

## Environmental Factors

Living environment profoundly shapes aging trajectories. Walkable neighborhoods encourage physical activity. Access to nutritious food supports dietary quality. Safe, affordable housing reduces chronic stress. Social infrastructure—community centers, libraries, parks—supports engagement.

### Purpose and Meaning

Viktor Frankl's observation that meaning sustains resilience has been validated in aging research. Older adults with a strong sense of purpose show lower rates of cognitive decline, cardiovascular events, and disability, even after controlling for baseline health status and socioeconomic factors.
