# Preventive Care and Health Screening

## Overview

Preventive care shifts healthcare focus from treating established disease to intercepting disease before it causes harm. Age-appropriate screenings, immunizations, and lifestyle counseling collectively prevent more disability-adjusted life years than most therapeutic interventions in clinical medicine.

## Screening Recommendations

Screening guidelines are based on the balance between the benefit of early detection and the harms of false positives, overdiagnosis, and invasive follow-up. Major guideline bodies including the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force publish evidence-based recommendations updated regularly.

### Cardiovascular Screening

Blood pressure measurement remains the highest-yield screening intervention across age groups. Hypertension affects nearly half of American adults and is often asymptomatic. Fasting lipid panels identify dyslipidemia that increases cardiovascular risk. Both conditions respond well to lifestyle intervention and, when needed, pharmacotherapy.

### Cancer Screening

Colorectal cancer screening via colonoscopy or stool-based testing is recommended beginning at age 45 for average-risk individuals. Breast cancer screening recommendations are nuanced, balancing detection benefits against overdiagnosis. Lung cancer screening with low-dose CT is recommended for long-term heavy smokers aged 50 to 80.

## Immunization

Vaccines prevent serious illness in older adults whose immune systems are less capable of mounting rapid responses to novel pathogens. Annual influenza vaccination, updated COVID-19 vaccines, shingles vaccination (Shingrix), and pneumococcal vaccination are key recommendations for adults over 65.

### Medication Reviews

Polypharmacy—taking five or more medications—is common in older adults and increases risks of drug interactions, falls, and adverse effects. Annual medication reviews with a pharmacist or physician identify opportunities to deprescribe unnecessary medications.

## Advance Care Planning

Preventive care encompasses planning for future health decisions. Advance directives, healthcare proxy designations, and goals-of-care conversations ensure that treatment preferences are honored even when individuals cannot communicate their wishes directly.
