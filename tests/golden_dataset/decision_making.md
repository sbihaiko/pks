# Decision Making Frameworks

## Overview

Decision making is the process of selecting a course of action from available alternatives. Good decision processes consistently produce better outcomes than poor ones, even though any individual decision can go wrong due to uncertainty. Improving decision quality requires understanding cognitive biases, using structured frameworks, and distinguishing between reversible and irreversible decisions.

## Cognitive Biases in Decisions

Availability bias causes us to overweight vivid, recent, or easily recalled examples when estimating probabilities. Confirmation bias leads us to seek evidence that supports our existing view and discount contradictory evidence. Sunk cost fallacy causes continued investment in failing projects because of prior investment rather than future prospects. Anchoring makes the first number heard disproportionately influential on subsequent estimates.

## Decision Frameworks

### Pre-Mortem Analysis

A pre-mortem imagines that the decision has been implemented and failed catastrophically, then works backward to identify what went wrong. This structured pessimism surfaces risks that optimism bias suppresses during normal planning. It is most effective when conducted with a group, as different people imagine different failure modes.

### Decision Matrix

A decision matrix scores alternatives against weighted criteria. Listing the criteria, weighting them by importance, and scoring each alternative makes the reasoning explicit and auditable. The process of building the matrix is often more valuable than the final score, as it forces clarity about what actually matters.

### Two-Way Door vs. One-Way Door

Reversible decisions (two-way doors) should be made quickly at the lowest appropriate level. Irreversible decisions (one-way doors) merit slower, more deliberate processes with more senior involvement. Mistaking a two-way door for a one-way door produces organizational paralysis; mistaking a one-way door for a two-way door produces costly mistakes.

## Deciding Under Uncertainty

When information is incomplete, decisions should be treated as bets. Assigning probability estimates rather than binary right/wrong judgments forces calibration. Keeping a decision log with the expected outcomes, the actual outcomes, and the quality of the decision process — independent of outcome — builds decision-making skill over time.
