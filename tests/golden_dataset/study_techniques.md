# Evidence-Based Study Techniques

## Overview

Not all study methods are equally effective. Decades of cognitive psychology research have identified a small number of techniques with robust evidence for improving long-term retention and transfer of knowledge. Unfortunately, the most commonly used techniques — re-reading and highlighting — rank among the least effective.

## High-Efficacy Techniques

### Spaced Repetition

Spaced repetition exploits the spacing effect: reviewing material at increasing intervals produces stronger memory traces than massed practice. Flashcard systems like Anki implement a spaced repetition algorithm (SM-2) that schedules cards based on previous recall performance. Studying with Anki for 20 minutes per day produces dramatically better retention than cramming the night before an exam.

### Retrieval Practice

Retrieval practice — actively recalling information from memory rather than passively reviewing it — strengthens memory traces more than re-reading. The testing effect is robust across ages, materials, and domains. Self-testing with flashcards, practice exams, or free recall after reading is more effective than re-reading the same material.

### Interleaving

Interleaving mixes different topics or problem types within a single study session rather than blocking by subject. Although interleaving feels harder and produces worse immediate performance, it improves long-term retention and the ability to discriminate between problem types.

## Elaborative Interrogation

Explaining why a fact is true and connecting it to existing knowledge produces richer encoding. Asking "why?" and "how does this relate to what I already know?" during study converts isolated facts into interconnected schemas.

## Study Environment

Consistent study environments and elimination of distractions — especially smartphones — improve focus and recall. Background music without lyrics has a small positive effect for some individuals; lyrical music impairs reading comprehension. Regular breaks following the Pomodoro technique or similar interval methods maintain sustained attention better than marathon sessions.
