# Fermentation for Home Kitchens

## Overview

Fermentation uses microorganisms — bacteria, yeasts, and molds — to transform food through enzymatic activity. Far from spoilage, fermentation creates new flavors, improves digestibility, synthesizes nutrients, and preserves food. Lacto-fermentation, wild yeast fermentation, and vinegar production are accessible to any home cook with basic equipment.

## Lacto-Fermentation

Lacto-fermentation relies on Lactobacillus bacteria, naturally present on vegetables, to produce lactic acid in an anaerobic environment. The salt concentration in the brine creates a selective environment that favors Lactobacillus while suppressing pathogens. Sauerkraut, kimchi, fermented pickles, and kvass are made by this method.

### Making Sauerkraut

Shredded cabbage is combined with 2% of its weight in non-iodized salt and massaged until it releases enough liquid to submerge itself. The mixture is packed tightly into a jar, ensuring the cabbage stays submerged below the brine. Fermentation occurs at room temperature over one to four weeks, depending on temperature and desired sourness.

## Wild Yeast Fermentation

Sourdough bread relies on a culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria maintained in a flour-and-water starter. The starter requires regular feeding — discarding part and adding fresh flour and water — to keep the microbial community active and balanced. Wild yeast produces carbon dioxide for leavening; lactic acid bacteria produce the characteristic sour flavor.

### Sourdough Starter Maintenance

A healthy starter doubles in volume within four to eight hours of feeding, smells pleasantly sour and yeasty, and shows abundant bubbles throughout. Hooch — a gray liquid that separates on the surface — indicates hunger, not spoilage; stir it back in or pour it off before feeding.

## Kombucha

Kombucha is sweetened tea fermented by a SCOBY (symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast). The SCOBY consumes sugar and produces a lightly effervescent, acidic beverage. Secondary fermentation in sealed bottles with added fruit juice or ginger builds carbonation. pH below 3.5 and proper sanitation prevent contamination.

## Safety Considerations

Unlike canning, lacto-fermentation and sourdough are self-protecting through acidification. Off smells — putrid, cheesy, or strongly alcoholic — indicate contamination. Mold growth above the brine surface should be removed; mold below the brine is far less common due to the anaerobic environment.
