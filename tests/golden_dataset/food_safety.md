# Food Safety Fundamentals

## Overview

Food safety practices prevent foodborne illness, which affects millions of people annually and ranges from mild discomfort to life-threatening infection. The causative organisms — Salmonella, E. coli O157:H7, Listeria, Campylobacter, Clostridium botulinum — are invisible, odorless, and flavorless. Food that smells and looks fine can cause severe illness; food that smells off is not necessarily unsafe.

## Temperature Danger Zone

Bacteria multiply most rapidly between 40°F and 140°F (4°C and 60°C) — the temperature danger zone. Foods should not remain in this range for more than two hours total (one hour when ambient temperature exceeds 90°F). Cooling hot foods quickly from 140°F to 40°F within four hours prevents dangerous proliferation during cooling.

### Safe Cooking Temperatures

Poultry must reach an internal temperature of 165°F (74°C) to eliminate Salmonella and Campylobacter. Ground beef reaches safety at 160°F (71°C). Whole cuts of beef, pork, lamb, and veal are safe at 145°F (63°C) with a three-minute rest. Fish is safe at 145°F. These temperatures are measured at the thickest point of the food, away from bone.

## Cross-Contamination Prevention

Raw meat, poultry, and seafood harbor pathogens that transfer to ready-to-eat foods through direct contact or shared surfaces. Dedicated cutting boards for raw proteins versus produce prevent this. Washing hands with soap for at least 20 seconds after handling raw meat is more important than any other single food safety practice.

### Refrigerator Storage Hierarchy

Raw poultry belongs on the lowest refrigerator shelf to prevent drip contamination of items below. Raw beef and pork occupy the next level. Ready-to-eat foods — leftovers, deli meats, cooked items — belong on upper shelves where they cannot receive drips from raw proteins.

## Leftovers and Reheating

Leftovers should be cooled to refrigerator temperature within two hours and consumed within three to four days. Reheating to 165°F kills most bacteria that grew during storage. The "smell test" is unreliable — odorless pathogens like C. perfringens can multiply in food that appears and smells perfectly normal.
