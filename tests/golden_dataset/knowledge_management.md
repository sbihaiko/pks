# Knowledge Management Systems

## Overview

Knowledge management is the systematic process of capturing, organizing, and making accessible the knowledge an individual or organization has accumulated. Without deliberate management, knowledge dissipates when people leave projects, memories fade, and notes become unsearchable archives.

## Personal Knowledge Management

### Capture and Inbox

All incoming information — articles, ideas, meeting notes, book highlights — is captured into a single inbox without judgment. Capture is separate from organization; trying to file perfectly at the moment of capture creates friction that reduces capture rate.

### Processing

Periodically, the inbox is processed. Each item is either discarded, acted on immediately, or moved to a reference system with appropriate tags and links. The goal is an empty inbox, not a full archive. The question for each item is: what is the actionable information here, and where will I need to find it?

### Linking Ideas

Personal knowledge management systems like Roam Research, Obsidian, and Logseq support bidirectional linking between notes. Linking related ideas surfaces non-obvious connections and builds a personal knowledge graph. Over time, clusters of linked notes reveal patterns invisible in a flat folder structure.

## Organizational Knowledge Management

Organizations face the challenge of converting tacit knowledge — expertise held in individuals' heads — into explicit knowledge that can be documented and shared. Communities of practice, structured retrospectives, and pair working sessions transfer tacit knowledge. Documentation, wikis, and process libraries store explicit knowledge.

### Search and Retrieval

A knowledge base is only valuable if its contents are findable. Full-text search with BM25 ranking is the baseline. Faceted filtering by tag, author, and date accelerates navigation for large repositories. Knowledge graphs surface conceptually related content even when keyword overlap is low.

## Knowledge Decay

Knowledge that is not reviewed decays. Outdated documentation is worse than no documentation because it misleads rather than informs. Regular knowledge base audits flag stale content for update or removal. Embedding documentation maintenance into project workflows — updating docs as part of completing a feature — prevents decay at the source.
