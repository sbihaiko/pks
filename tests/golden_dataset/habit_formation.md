# Habit Formation Science

## Overview

Habits are automatic behaviors triggered by contextual cues. They encode frequently repeated actions in procedural memory, reducing the cognitive load of routine. Understanding the mechanisms of habit formation enables deliberate design of positive habits and systematic removal of harmful ones.

## The Habit Loop

The habit loop consists of three elements: cue, routine, and reward. The cue triggers the routine; the reward reinforces it, strengthening the association between cue and routine. The basal ganglia stores habit memories independent of the hippocampus, which is why habits persist even after amnesia. Changing a habit requires identifying its cue and reward and substituting a different routine, not simply trying to suppress the old one.

## Building New Habits

### Implementation Intentions

Implementation intentions specify when, where, and how a new behavior will be performed: "When X happens, I will do Y." This specificity converts an abstract intention into a concrete plan triggered by a particular context. Research shows implementation intentions roughly double the rate of habit formation compared to goal setting alone.

### Habit Stacking

Habit stacking anchors a new habit to an existing one: "After I do X, I will do Y." The existing habit provides a reliable cue without requiring a new contextual trigger. Chains of stacked habits can build entire morning or evening routines incrementally.

### Minimum Viable Habit

Starting with the smallest possible version of a desired habit reduces activation energy. A two-minute rule — any habit can be started in two minutes — bypasses the resistance that prevents starting. Once started, continuation is far more likely. The two-minute version gradually extends once the cue-routine-reward loop is established.

## Breaking Unwanted Habits

Increasing friction around undesired habits reduces their frequency. Removing cues eliminates the trigger. Adding steps between cue and routine lengthens the decision window, allowing competing behaviors to compete. Environmental redesign — not relying on willpower — is more sustainable because it operates automatically.

## Tracking and Streaks

Habit tracking creates a visual record of consistency that itself becomes a motivating cue. The "don't break the chain" effect of streaks motivates continuation. However, streak obsession can create rigidity; missing one day should not cause complete abandonment (the abstinence violation effect).
