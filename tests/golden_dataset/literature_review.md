# Conducting a Literature Review

## Overview

A literature review maps the existing body of knowledge relevant to a research question. It identifies what is known, what is contested, and where gaps exist. A thorough, well-organized literature review establishes the scholarly context that justifies new research and prevents duplication of effort.

## Search Strategy

A systematic search uses multiple databases — PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, ACM Digital Library — with controlled vocabulary terms and Boolean operators. Documenting the search strategy in a PRISMA flow diagram ensures reproducibility. Hand-searching reference lists of key papers catches studies that database indexing misses.

### Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Criteria must be defined before screening begins to prevent confirmation bias. Typical criteria restrict by publication date, study design, population, intervention, and outcome. Two independent reviewers screen titles and abstracts, then full texts. Disagreements are resolved through discussion or a third reviewer.

## Synthesizing Evidence

### Narrative Synthesis

Narrative synthesis organizes findings thematically, comparing and contrasting what studies found. It is appropriate when studies are too heterogeneous to combine statistically. Good narrative synthesis goes beyond description to interpret the pattern of evidence.

### Meta-Analysis

Meta-analysis statistically combines effect sizes from multiple studies to produce a pooled estimate with narrower confidence intervals than any individual study. Heterogeneity statistics (I² ) quantify inconsistency across studies. Forest plots visualize individual and pooled effects. Funnel plots detect publication bias.

## Staying Current

A literature review is never truly finished. Setting up citation alerts and database search alerts ensures new publications are captured as the field advances. Zotero and Mendeley automate reference management and facilitate sharing libraries across research teams.

## Writing the Review

A well-structured review introduces the research question, explains the search and screening process, synthesizes findings thematically or chronologically, identifies gaps, and proposes future research directions. Critical appraisal — evaluating study quality, not just summarizing findings — distinguishes a systematic review from an annotated bibliography.
