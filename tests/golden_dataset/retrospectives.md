# Personal Retrospectives

## Overview

A retrospective is a structured reflection on a past period of work or life with the goal of identifying what worked, what did not, and what to change going forward. Team retrospectives are a cornerstone of Agile development; personal retrospectives apply the same logic to individual practice, producing deliberate improvement rather than accidental stagnation.

## Retrospective Formats

### Start-Stop-Continue

The simplest retrospective format asks three questions: What should I start doing? What should I stop doing? What should I continue doing? The symmetry is important — identifying behaviors to continue avoids the trap of retrospectives that produce only lists of problems without acknowledging strengths.

### Four Ls: Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed For

This format adds nuance to the simple stop-start-continue. Liked captures positive experiences. Learned captures new understanding. Lacked identifies missing resources or skills. Longed For captures desired experiences that did not happen. The four-quadrant format produces richer reflection than a binary good/bad assessment.

### Mad-Sad-Glad

An emotional lens surfaces affective dimensions of experience that behavioral analysis misses. What made me mad? What made me sad? What made me glad? Emotional patterns across retrospectives reveal value misalignments: persistent sadness around certain tasks signals a mismatch between work and values.

## Cadence and Timing

Weekly retrospectives cover tactical execution. Monthly retrospectives review progress on longer-term goals and patterns across weeks. Quarterly retrospectives assess strategic direction and recalibrate annual goals. Annual retrospectives provide the broadest perspective, comparing this year to previous years and evaluating life trajectory.

## Acting on Retrospective Findings

A retrospective that produces insights but no changes is better than nothing but far below its potential. Each retrospective should produce at most two or three specific, concrete behavior changes with implementation intentions attached. More than that overwhelms capacity for change; fewer wastes the reflection.

## Archiving Retrospectives

Keeping a written record of retrospectives enables meta-retrospection: reviewing patterns across multiple retrospectives reveals whether identified problems are genuinely being addressed or recurring without change.
