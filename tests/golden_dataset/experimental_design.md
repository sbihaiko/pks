# Experimental Design Principles

## Overview

Experimental design is the blueprint for a study — decisions made before data collection that determine what causal claims the data can support. A well-designed experiment controls confounding, maximizes statistical power, and produces interpretable results. Poor design cannot be remedied by sophisticated analysis.

## Core Principles

### Randomization

Random assignment of participants to conditions is the defining feature of a true experiment. Randomization distributes known and unknown confounders evenly across conditions, making the groups comparable on everything except the manipulated variable. Without randomization, observed differences may reflect pre-existing group differences rather than treatment effects.

### Control Conditions

A control condition provides the baseline against which treatment effects are measured. In drug trials, the placebo control accounts for the therapeutic effect of receiving treatment. Waitlist controls allow comparison while ensuring all participants eventually receive the intervention. Active controls compare two treatments head-to-head rather than treatment against no treatment.

### Blinding

Single-blinding conceals condition assignment from participants, preventing demand characteristics and placebo effects. Double-blinding also conceals assignment from those administering the treatment and collecting outcomes, preventing experimenter bias. Triple-blinding extends concealment to those analyzing the data.

## Experimental Designs

### Between-Subjects Design

Different participants are assigned to each condition. Individual differences contribute noise. Large samples are needed for adequate power. Participant attrition is a threat when dropouts are systematically different from completers.

### Within-Subjects Design

The same participants receive all conditions. Each participant serves as their own control, reducing noise from individual differences. Carryover effects — where one condition affects performance in a subsequent condition — are managed through counterbalancing the order of conditions.

### Factorial Design

Factorial designs manipulate multiple independent variables simultaneously. A 2×2 factorial crosses two variables with two levels each, producing four conditions. This design tests not only main effects but interactions — whether the effect of one variable depends on the level of another.

## Statistical Power

Power is the probability of detecting a true effect. It depends on effect size, sample size, and significance threshold. An underpowered study wastes resources and produces unreliable estimates. Power analysis before data collection determines the required sample size.
