# Seasonal and Local Eating

## Overview

Seasonal eating aligns food choices with the natural production cycles of local agriculture. Ingredients at peak seasonal ripeness are more flavorful, more nutritious, and less expensive than out-of-season equivalents shipped from distant climates. Eating seasonally also reduces the environmental cost of refrigerated long-distance transport.

## Understanding Seasonal Produce

### Spring Produce

Spring brings the first tender produce after winter's root vegetables: asparagus, peas, fava beans, spring onions, radishes, arugula, and early lettuces. These ingredients are sweet and delicate because they have not yet developed the fibrous texture that heat and maturity bring. They require minimal cooking and complement each other in light salads and pasta dishes.

### Summer Abundance

Summer is the peak of abundance: tomatoes, corn, zucchini, cucumbers, peppers, eggplant, stone fruits, and berries. These ingredients are at their most flavorful at room temperature, not refrigerated. Refrigerating ripe tomatoes, in particular, destroys the volatile compounds responsible for their characteristic flavor.

### Autumn and Winter

Autumn brings hard squash, sweet potatoes, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower, and late-season apples and pears. Root vegetables — carrots, parsnips, celeriac, turnips — become sweeter after frost converts stored starches to sugars. Winter eating focuses on preserved and stored foods as well as cold-hardy brassicas.

## Local Food Systems

Farmers' markets provide direct access to regional producers and often sell varieties bred for flavor rather than the durability required for long-distance shipping. Community-supported agriculture (CSA) shares distribute a weekly selection of whatever is harvested that week, introducing members to unfamiliar varieties and building cooking flexibility.

## Preservation for Off-Season Use

Seasonal eating does not require foregoing summer tomatoes in January. Preserving seasonal abundance — freezing berries, canning tomatoes, fermenting cabbage, drying herbs — extends peak-season quality into the off-season. The labor invested in summer preservation pays dividends in winter when local fresh produce is scarce.
