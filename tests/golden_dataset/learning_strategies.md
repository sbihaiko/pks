# Effective Learning Strategies

## Overview

Learning — the acquisition of new knowledge and skills — is itself a learnable skill. Understanding how memory and skill formation work allows deliberate selection of learning strategies that produce durable knowledge rather than the illusion of learning that passive review creates.

## Principles of Effective Learning

### Desirable Difficulty

Conditions that make learning harder in the short term often produce better long-term retention. Retrieval practice, spaced repetition, and interleaving feel less productive than re-reading and massed practice, but produce significantly better outcomes at delayed testing. The feeling of fluency during study is often a poor proxy for actual learning.

### Elaboration

Elaboration involves explaining material in your own words, connecting it to prior knowledge, and generating examples. Asking "why does this work?" and "when would this apply?" deepens encoding. Teaching material to others (the Feynman technique) is a powerful form of elaboration because gaps in understanding are immediately exposed.

## Skill Acquisition

### Deliberate Practice

Deliberate practice targets specific weaknesses at the edge of current capability, incorporates immediate feedback, and requires full attention. It differs from naive practice (repeating what you can already do) and from performance (executing without learning intent). Expert performance in any domain is the accumulated result of thousands of hours of deliberate practice, not talent.

### Chunking

Experts perceive larger, more meaningful patterns (chunks) where novices see isolated elements. Chess masters recognize board configurations, not individual piece positions. Programmers recognize design patterns, not individual lines. Building chunks requires sufficient practice for recognition to become automatic.

## Learning Planning

Identifying the minimal viable skill set for a goal, then sequencing learning to build foundational knowledge before advanced applications, avoids the common mistake of studying advanced material without the prerequisites to understand it. Meta-learning — studying how to learn the subject — reduces total time to competence.

## Managing Cognitive Load

Intrinsic load is the inherent complexity of the material. Extraneous load is unnecessary cognitive demand from poor instructional design. Germane load is the productive effort of constructing schemas. Effective learning materials minimize extraneous load so available cognitive capacity can be directed toward germane load.
