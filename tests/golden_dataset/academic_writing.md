# Academic Writing Skills

## Overview

Academic writing communicates research findings and scholarly arguments to a disciplinary audience. It is precise, evidence-based, and impersonal in register. Mastering academic writing conventions enables participation in scholarly discourse and ensures ideas are taken seriously regardless of their novelty.

## Structure and Argumentation

Academic papers follow a logical structure: introduction, methods, results, discussion, and conclusion (IMRAD) for empirical work; introduction, argument, counter-argument, conclusion for theoretical work. Every paragraph advances one claim, supported by evidence, and connects to the preceding and following paragraphs through explicit transitions.

### The Thesis Statement

The thesis statement summarizes the central argument in one or two sentences. It should be specific, debatable, and supported by the body of the paper. A weak thesis merely states a fact; a strong thesis makes a claim that requires evidence and reasoning to sustain.

## Citation and Attribution

All borrowed ideas, data, and quotations must be attributed. Failing to attribute borrowed material constitutes plagiarism. Direct quotations are used sparingly — to capture distinctive phrasing or authoritative statements — and paraphrase is preferred. APA, MLA, and Chicago are common citation styles; follow the one required by the target journal or institution.

## Hedging Language

Academic writing uses hedging to calibrate claims to the strength of evidence. "This study suggests" is more accurate than "this study proves." "A majority of participants" is more precise than "participants generally." Hedging is not weakness — it is epistemic honesty.

## Revision Process

First drafts prioritize getting ideas on the page; revision makes them clear and compelling. Reading aloud reveals awkward sentences. Peer review catches ambiguities invisible to the author. Professional editing is not cheating — it is a standard part of scholarly publication.

## Writing for Journals

Journal submission requires careful target selection based on scope, audience, and impact factor. The cover letter summarizes the contribution and explains why the paper fits the journal. Responding to reviewer comments systematically, point by point, increases acceptance probability.
