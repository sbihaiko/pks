# Kitchen Organization Principles

## Overview

Kitchen organization arranges tools, equipment, and ingredients so that the most frequently used items are the most accessible. An organized kitchen reduces the friction of cooking, makes inventory management easier, and prevents duplicate purchases of forgotten items. The goal is a system that maintains itself with minimal effort.

## Zone-Based Organization

The kitchen is divided into zones based on workflow: prep zone near the cutting board, cooking zone around the stove, baking zone near the oven, cleaning zone around the sink. Items are stored in the zone where they are used. Pots live near the stove; mixing bowls and measuring cups live near the baking zone. Eliminating cross-kitchen retrieval saves cumulative minutes every day of cooking.

### Frequently vs. Infrequently Used Items

Items used daily belong at arm's reach in prime real estate: counter space, front of shelves, lowest cabinet drawers. Items used weekly occupy accessible but not premium space. Items used once a year are stored out of the way — high shelves, back of cabinets, or off-site storage. Audit periodically and ruthlessly reassign.

## Pantry Organization

Grouping pantry items by category — baking supplies, canned goods, grains and legumes, condiments, snacks — allows finding items without searching. Decanting bulk dry goods into clear, labeled containers of uniform size enables stacking, makes quantities visible, and prevents the bag-within-a-bag chaos of poorly organized dry goods storage.

### FIFO Rotation

First-in, first-out rotation ensures older items are used before newer ones. Placing new purchases behind existing stock when restocking, rather than in front, enforces FIFO automatically without requiring active management.

## Refrigerator Organization

The coldest parts of the refrigerator — back of the middle and bottom shelves — are best for meat, fish, and dairy. The warmer door shelves are appropriate for condiments and butter. Produce drawers maintain higher humidity for vegetables and lower humidity for fruit. Clear containers and consistent placement make the refrigerator's contents visible without rummaging.

## Equipment Rationalization

Kitchen equipment proliferates easily and rarely gets discarded. Each item should be evaluated against frequency of use. A single-use gadget used twice a year occupies space that a frequently used tool could fill. Annual audits identify equipment that can be donated or discarded.
