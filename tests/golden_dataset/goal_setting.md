# Goal Setting Methods

## Overview

Goal setting translates ambition into action. Well-formed goals provide direction, motivate effort, and create a standard for evaluating progress. Poorly formed goals — vague aspirations without concrete criteria — provide the psychological feeling of planning without its practical benefits.

## SMART Goals

The SMART framework specifies that goals should be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. A specific goal names exactly what will be accomplished and by whom. A measurable goal has objective criteria for completion. An achievable goal is challenging but within reach given available resources. A relevant goal serves a larger purpose. A time-bound goal has a deadline that creates accountability.

## OKR Framework

Objectives and Key Results (OKRs) operate at two levels: aspirational objectives describe a qualitative destination, and key results are three to five measurable outcomes that define what achieving the objective looks like. OKRs are typically set quarterly. Scoring key results on a 0–1 scale at the end of the quarter creates a feedback loop. Consistently scoring 0.7–0.8 indicates correctly calibrated ambition; consistently scoring 1.0 suggests goals were too easy.

## Long-Term Goal Planning

### Horizon Mapping

Mapping goals across multiple time horizons — one year, three years, ten years — ensures that near-term goals are aligned with long-term direction. The ten-year vision prevents the one-year plan from being purely reactive. Reviewing the long-term vision when making significant decisions prevents short-term optimization at the expense of long-term direction.

### Backward Planning

Starting from a desired future state and working backward to the present reveals the sequence of steps required and makes the path from here to there concrete. This is more effective than forward planning, which tends to underestimate obstacles and overestimate early motivation.

## Review and Adjustment

Goals should be reviewed weekly at the tactical level and monthly at the strategic level. Goals that remain relevant but have become impossible due to changed circumstances should be revised rather than abandoned. Distinguishing between giving up on a goal and intelligently pivoting based on new information is a critical metacognitive skill.
