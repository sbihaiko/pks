# Hydration and Fluid Balance

## Overview

Water constitutes approximately 60% of total body weight and participates in every physiological process, including nutrient transport, temperature regulation, joint lubrication, and waste elimination. Even mild dehydration—as little as 1-2% of body weight—impairs cognitive performance, physical endurance, and mood.

## Hydration Needs

Daily fluid requirements vary based on body size, activity level, climate, and health status. A general guideline for adults is 2.7 liters per day for women and 3.7 liters per day for men from all sources, including beverages and food.

### Aging and Thirst Perception

Older adults face elevated dehydration risk because the thirst mechanism becomes less sensitive with age. This means relying on thirst alone is an unreliable indicator of hydration status. Scheduled fluid intake throughout the day—rather than waiting for thirst—is a more reliable strategy.

## Assessing Hydration Status

Urine color is a practical hydration indicator. Pale yellow urine indicates adequate hydration; dark amber signals dehydration. Monitoring urine output frequency—aiming for four to six voids per day—provides an additional behavioral cue.

### Factors That Increase Needs

Certain conditions and medications dramatically increase fluid requirements:

- Diuretic medications common in cardiac and hypertension management
- Diabetes, which increases urinary output
- High environmental temperatures or fever
- Diarrhea or vomiting during illness

## Beverage Choices

Water remains the optimal hydration fluid. Low-fat milk provides fluid alongside protein and calcium. Herbal teas contribute hydration with minimal caloric impact. Coffee and tea, despite mild diuretic effects, contribute net fluid when consumed in moderate quantities.

### Reducing Dehydrating Beverages

Alcohol is a potent diuretic that increases urinary losses beyond the volume consumed. Sugary beverages contribute calories and may worsen insulin resistance without providing superior hydration compared to water.
