# Energy Management for Peak Performance

## Overview

Time is fixed; energy is renewable but limited within each day. Managing energy — physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual — allows high performance to be sustained over years rather than burned out in months. The performance pyramid builds from physical foundation upward, with each layer depending on those below.

## Physical Energy

Physical energy is the foundation of all performance. It is generated and replenished through sleep, nutrition, movement, and recovery. Sleep debt accumulates and impairs cognitive function, emotional regulation, and physical performance. The 7 to 9 hour recommendation for adults is the evidence-based norm, not an aspirational ideal.

### Movement and Energy

Regular aerobic exercise increases mitochondrial density, improves insulin sensitivity, and elevates baseline energy levels. Even brief movement breaks — 10 minutes of walking — restore attention and reduce the cognitive fatigue that accumulates during sustained desk work. Sedentary work without movement breaks produces an energy debt by afternoon that caffeine merely masks.

## Mental Energy

Mental energy is consumed by sustained concentration, decision making, and emotional labor. Cognitive tasks that are novel, complex, or emotionally charged deplete mental energy faster than routine tasks. Scheduling the most demanding cognitive work during peak energy periods — typically mid-morning for most chronotypes — maximizes output quality.

### Decision Fatigue

The quality of decisions degrades throughout the day as mental energy is depleted. Reducing the number of trivial decisions — standardizing routines, automating choices, delegating — preserves mental energy for high-stakes decisions. Barack Obama wore the same style of suit daily to eliminate one trivial decision; the principle applies at any scale.

## Emotional Energy

Emotional energy is consumed by interpersonal conflict, performance anxiety, and suppressing authentic emotional responses. Recovery practices — solitude, nature, creative activities — replenish it. Identifying the specific interactions and environments that drain emotional energy allows deliberate management rather than reactive depletion.

## Recovery Rituals

Recovery is not laziness — it is an essential input to sustained performance. Full disengagement from work during recovery periods produces better subsequent performance than partial engagement. Vacations, weekends, and daily rest periods all contribute to resilience when they involve genuine recovery rather than low-grade work continuation.
