# Food Preservation Methods

## Overview

Food preservation extends the edible life of ingredients by inhibiting microbial growth, enzymatic activity, and oxidation. Traditional preservation methods — drying, salting, curing, fermenting, and pickling — were developed over millennia before refrigeration existed. Modern home cooks use them for flavor development as much as necessity.

## Temperature-Based Preservation

### Refrigeration

Refrigeration slows microbial growth by cooling food to 35°F to 38°F (2°C to 3°C). Most pathogens grow slowly at refrigerator temperatures; cold does not sterilize food. The refrigerator door, which fluctuates in temperature, is inappropriate for dairy and eggs. First-in, first-out rotation prevents older items from being buried behind newer ones.

### Freezing

Freezing halts microbial growth entirely but does not kill pathogens — they resume growth when food thaws. Blanching vegetables before freezing deactivates enzymes that cause off-flavors and texture degradation. Vacuum sealing before freezing eliminates freezer burn by removing the air that causes oxidation. Most cooked foods freeze well for three to six months; quality degrades progressively beyond that.

## Dehydration

Removing moisture below the water activity threshold required for microbial growth preserves food at room temperature. Dehydrators use low heat and airflow; ovens at their lowest setting approximate this. Jerky, dried herbs, sun-dried tomatoes, and dried fruit are common home dehydration products.

## Salt and Acid Preservation

Salt draws moisture out of food through osmosis and inhibits microbial growth at sufficient concentrations. Dry curing uses salt directly; brining suspends food in a salt solution. Vinegar pickling uses acetic acid to create an environment hostile to most pathogens. The USDA recommends minimum acidity levels for safe home pickling.

## Canning

Water bath canning is appropriate for high-acid foods (pH below 4.6) including most fruits, jams, and pickles. Pressure canning is required for low-acid foods (vegetables, meats, beans) to reach the temperatures necessary to eliminate Clostridium botulinum spores. Following tested recipes exactly is essential — home experimentation with canning ratios can produce dangerous results.
