# Meal Planning for Home Cooks

## Overview

Meal planning is the practice of deciding what to cook before shopping, eliminating the daily "what's for dinner?" decision and its associated stress. A functional meal plan reduces food waste, saves money by preventing impulse purchases, and makes eating well easier because healthy options are available and prepared.

## Building a Weekly Plan

### Inventory First

Before planning, assess what is already in the pantry, refrigerator, and freezer. Planning around existing ingredients reduces waste and prevents accumulating duplicates. Shelf-stable pantry items with approaching expiration dates should anchor the week's meals.

### Recipe Selection

Selecting recipes with overlapping ingredients reduces the variety of items purchased and the risk of buying a specialty ingredient used once and discarded. A chicken thigh bought for one recipe can be used in tacos, grain bowls, and soup across the week. Planning a "use it up" meal on day six or seven converts any remaining perishables into a meal rather than waste.

## Preparation and Batch Cooking

Batch cooking — preparing components in large quantities during a dedicated session — reduces weekday cooking time dramatically. Cooking a large pot of grains, roasting two sheet pans of vegetables, and making a large pot of beans or a protein takes two to three hours on Sunday and makes weeknight assembly a 15-minute task rather than an hour-long production.

### Mise en Place

Washing, cutting, and storing vegetables at the time of purchase, rather than when cooking begins, reduces the friction of weeknight cooking. When carrots are already julienned in the refrigerator, they are used; when they require washing and cutting at the moment of hunger, they are often bypassed for something faster.

## Shopping List Management

Organizing the shopping list by store section — produce, dairy, meat, dry goods — reduces backtracking and time in the store. Digital lists shared between household members prevent duplication. Buying in bulk for non-perishable staples used regularly (olive oil, canned tomatoes, dried lentils) reduces unit cost and shopping frequency.
