# Sleep Hygiene for Older Adults

## Overview

Quality sleep is a foundational pillar of health, especially as we age. Adults over 65 typically need seven to eight hours of sleep per night, yet many report difficulty falling or staying asleep. Poor sleep hygiene is one of the most correctable contributors to this problem.

## Core Principles

Establishing a consistent sleep schedule anchors the body's circadian rhythm. Going to bed and waking at the same time every day—including weekends—trains the brain to release melatonin at predictable intervals.

### Light Exposure

Morning sunlight exposure within an hour of waking is one of the strongest signals for resetting the circadian clock. Conversely, blue light from screens in the two hours before bedtime suppresses melatonin production and delays sleep onset.

## Environmental Factors

The bedroom environment profoundly affects sleep quality. Optimal conditions include:

- Room temperature between 65°F and 68°F (18°C–20°C)
- Darkness achieved through blackout curtains or a sleep mask
- Low ambient noise or consistent masking sound such as white noise

### Mattress and Posture

An unsupportive mattress can cause musculoskeletal discomfort that fragments sleep. Side sleeping with a pillow between the knees reduces lumbar strain and is recommended for people with lower back concerns.

## Pre-Sleep Routine

A wind-down routine signals the nervous system that sleep is approaching. Effective routines last 30 to 60 minutes and may include gentle stretching, reading physical books, or a warm bath. The drop in core body temperature after a warm bath accelerates sleep onset.

### Substances to Avoid

Caffeine has a half-life of five to seven hours, meaning a cup of coffee at 3 p.m. leaves half its stimulant effect active at 8 p.m. Alcohol, though sedating initially, suppresses REM sleep and causes fragmented rest in the second half of the night.

## Daytime Habits

Regular physical activity improves sleep quality, but vigorous exercise within three hours of bedtime can delay sleep onset. Short naps of 20 minutes before 3 p.m. restore alertness without disrupting nighttime sleep.
